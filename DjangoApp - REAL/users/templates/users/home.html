What is SEPTMUST?





Social engineering attacks focusing on university students specifically refer to the manipulation and deception techniques used by attackers to exploit vulnerabilities in students' trust, curiosity, and reliance on digital platforms within the university environment. These attacks aim to deceive students and gain unauthorized access to sensitive information, personal data, financial resources, or even compromise university systems.




  • Phishing is one of the most prevalent social engineering techniques. Attackers send deceptive emails, messages, or even phone calls that appear to be from trusted sources, such as a university's IT department or a popular online service. The messages often urge students to click on malicious links, provide login credentials, or disclose personal information.

  • Vishing, short for "voice phishing," is a social engineering technique where attackers use voice calls to deceive and manipulate individuals into revealing sensitive information or performing certain actions. In the context of university students, vishing attacks may involve receiving phone calls from someone impersonating a trusted entity, such as a university staff member, a financial institution, or a technical support representative.

  • Pretexting involves creating a false scenario or story to trick students into revealing confidential information. For example, an attacker might pose as a university staff member conducting a survey or a research study, requesting personal details under the guise of a legitimate purpose.

  • Impersonation attacks is where attackers may impersonate authority figures or trusted individuals to gain students' trust and manipulate them into sharing sensitive information or performing certain actions. They might pretend to be a professor, a university administrator, or a fellow student to deceive their targets.

  • Baiting refers to the use of enticing digital content or offers to trick students into engaging with malicious links or downloads. The bait is typically presented through online platforms such as email, social media, or websites loaded with malicious software, such as malware or keyloggers, which can compromise the student's device and provide unauthorized access to sensitive information..
