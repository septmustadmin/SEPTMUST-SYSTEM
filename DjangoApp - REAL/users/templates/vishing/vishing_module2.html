Module 2: Identifying Potential Vishing Targets




In the context of university students, vishing attacks may involve receiving phone calls from someone impersonating a trusted entity, such as a university staff member, a financial institution, or a technical support representative. The attacker may use various tactics, such as creating a sense of urgency, instilling fear, or offering enticing rewards, to trick students into disclosing their personal information, such as passwords, social security numbers, or banking details.


2.1 Identifying Potential Vishing Targets: Information valuable to attackers, How attackers gather information about targets and Common sources of leaked information


2.1.1 Information valuable to attackers:

Attackers target individuals who possess valuable information that can be exploited for financial gain or other malicious purposes. Some examples of information that may be valuable to vishing attackers include:

Personal Identifiable Information (PII): This includes full names, addresses, phone numbers, Social Security numbers, dates of birth, and other details that can be used for identity theft or account takeover.

Financial Information: Attackers seek details such as credit card numbers, bank account numbers, PINs, and login credentials to gain unauthorized access to accounts or conduct fraudulent transactions.

Personal Relationships: Attackers may target individuals who have personal or professional relationships with individuals or organizations of interest. This information can be used for social engineering tactics to gain trust or manipulate victims.



2.1.2 How attackers gather information about targets:

Attackers employ various techniques to gather information about their potential targets. These techniques may include:

Data Breaches: Hackers frequently target organizations to gain access to databases containing sensitive customer information. If successful, attackers can obtain a wealth of personal and financial data that can be used in vishing attacks.

Social Engineering: Attackers use social engineering techniques to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information. They may engage in conversation, posing as a trustworthy entity, and manipulate victims into sharing personal details.

Online Sources: Attackers leverage publicly available information on social media platforms, professional networking sites, and other online sources to gather personal information about their targets. This information can be used to personalize their vishing attempts and make them appear more convincing.

Phishing Attacks: Attackers may use phishing emails or text messages to trick individuals into visiting malicious websites or downloading malware-infected attachments. These attacks can harvest personal information, including login credentials, which can then be used in vishing attempts.



2.1.3 Common sources of leaked information:

Data breaches: When organizations suffer a security breach, hackers can gain access to databases containing customer information. This information may include personal and financial details, which can be sold on the dark web or used in vishing attacks.

Public records: Certain personal information, such as addresses, phone numbers, and property ownership records, can be obtained through public records. Attackers can use this information to enhance the credibility of their vishing attempts.

Social media platforms: People often share a wealth of personal information on social media platforms, including their full names, birthdays, locations, and even details about their relationships. Attackers can mine this information to create targeted vishing attacks.

Data mining: Attackers can employ automated tools and techniques to scrape publicly available information from various online sources. This can include online directories, forums, websites, and any other platforms where individuals may have shared personal information.

Dumpster diving: Although less common in the digital age, attackers may still resort to physically searching through trash or recycling bins to find discarded documents containing valuable information, such as bank statements or credit card statements.
